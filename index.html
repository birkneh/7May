Why this block

Your SI joint dysfunction means anything that loads the hips axially or asymmetrically is off the table — squat, deadlift, lunge, hinge, even standing presses transmit force through the SI joint via the spinal column.

What you can still train hard: chest, back, shoulders, arms, and rear delts — with two non-negotiable rules.

  • Seated or chest-supported only. No standing pressing or rowing.
  • No spinal flexion under load. No cable crunches, sit-ups, weighted Russian twists.
  • No hanging exercises in early sessions. The traction can pull on irritated SI structures.

Walking is fine on rest days — keeps the hips mobile without loading them. Aim for 20–40 min easy pace. Avoid running, hiking, cycling on hills.

Loading & progression

Pick a starting weight you can lift for the bottom of the rep range with 1–2 reps in reserve. The session should feel productive but not maximal.

  • Week 1–2: stay light. Re-learn the exercises. Focus on form and the mind-muscle connection.
  • Week 3–6: add weight when you can complete all sets at the top of the rep range with clean form.
  • Week 7+: cycle progression — when you stop progressing weight, drop sets to 4 and bump reps; or vice versa.

Compound lifts (highlighted rows) are your priority. If energy fades, drop a set from the accessories — never from the main movements.

When to stop a session

Even with the avoidance list, some movements may still bother the joint. End the session if any of these happen.

  • Sharp pain anywhere from the lower back to the buttock during a set.
  • Pain that intensifies set over set rather than warming up.
  • Numbness, tingling, or shooting pain down the leg.
  • Difficulty walking normally after a movement.
A dull post-workout ache in the muscles you trained is normal. New or sharper pain in or near the SI joint is your signal to stop and reassess.
A note from Claude

I'm not a physiotherapist and this isn't a clinical prescription. This is a sensible training framework based on standard rehab-phase principles for SI joint issues, designed around the constraints you described. Run it past your physio when you see them — they may want to add specific stabilization or mobility work, or restrict additional movements. If anything in the program causes pain, stop and check with the physio before pushing through.
